Chapter 7

What Discernment Revealed

The lessons Jesus was teaching me did not arrive all at once. Looking back now, I can see that many of them emerged gradually, often through ordinary moments that seemed insignificant at the time. Rarely did clarity arrive through a single dramatic realization. More often, understanding developed one piece at a time until enough pieces existed for a larger picture to become visible.

For years, I had approached most difficult situations the same way. Whenever tension appeared, my attention immediately turned toward understanding what had happened. I examined conversations. I revisited details. I replayed events in my mind searching for explanations that might help everything make sense. Such habits were not entirely unhealthy. Honest self examination possesses value, and I believe every person benefits from periodically evaluating their own actions, attitudes, and responses.

The difficulty arose when every situation began with me and ended with me.

Questions followed me after disagreements. Questions followed me during quiet moments. Questions followed me into prayer. Could I have communicated more clearly? Could I have responded differently? Could I have prevented the situation from escalating? Those questions occupied far more space within my thoughts than I realized at the time.

Eventually, another question began emerging.

What happens when the same emotional outcome continues appearing despite different circumstances?

That question lingered in my thoughts long after it first appeared. I did not immediately know how to answer it. Part of me continued examining individual situations one at a time. Another part of me was beginning to notice something I had previously overlooked. The details surrounding certain conflicts often differed significantly from one another, yet the emotional destination remained remarkably similar.

The realization did not create anger.

The realization created pause.

For the first time, I found myself stepping back far enough to examine broader patterns rather than isolated moments. Looking at individual events had always provided explanations. Looking at patterns provided perspective. Both possessed value, though perspective revealed things that explanations alone could not.

As I reflected upon previous years, memories returned with greater clarity than before. Conversations that once seemed disconnected now appeared related. Incidents that once seemed isolated now appeared connected by recurring themes. Situations that once felt confusing began revealing similarities that had previously remained hidden beneath the details.

The process felt uncomfortable because clarity often requires honesty, and honesty sometimes reveals truths that challenge beliefs we have carried for years. I wanted to believe that every difficult moment existed entirely on its own. I wanted to believe that enough patience, enough grace, and enough understanding would eventually solve every recurring issue. Those desires came from a sincere place, though sincerity alone could not change what continued presenting itself before me.

Jesus was teaching me that recognizing a pattern is not the same thing as judging a person.

That distinction mattered.

For a long time, I worried that acknowledging certain realities might somehow make me less compassionate. The opposite proved true. Compassion rooted in truth becomes healthier than compassion rooted in denial because truth allows us to see situations clearly rather than through the lens of wishful thinking.

As my perspective changed, another realization quietly emerged.

Observing a pattern does not create the pattern.

Acknowledging a reality does not produce the reality.

Recognizing what exists does not make a person responsible for its existence.

Those truths brought a measure of freedom that I had not experienced in a very long time. Much of the weight I had been carrying came from assuming responsibility for matters that ultimately belonged to someone else. I had spent years attempting to understand, manage, prevent, or improve situations that extended beyond my control. Discernment gradually revealed the difference between caring about something and being responsible for it.

That lesson changed the way I viewed many aspects of my life.

The change was not dramatic. No lightning bolt appeared. No single conversation transformed everything overnight. The shift occurred quietly as Jesus continued guiding me toward greater clarity. Questions that once produced confusion began producing understanding. Burdens that once felt inseparable from love began revealing themselves as responsibilities that love was never intended to carry.

Looking back now, I can see that discernment was one of the greatest gifts Jesus provided during that season. Discernment did not remove every question, nor did it immediately solve every problem. What discernment provided was something I desperately needed.

It provided clarity.

And clarity, once received, made it increasingly difficult to confuse temporary calm with lasting peace.

That lesson would become one of the most important lessons Jesus had yet to teach me.
