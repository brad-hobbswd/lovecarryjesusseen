Chapter 2

The Familiar Feeling

The realization did not arrive all at once.

Long after the drive ended and the conversations had faded into silence, I found myself replaying the events of the day in my mind. Every attempt to understand what had happened led me back to the same conclusion. The strongest emotion I felt was not anger. The strongest emotion I felt was familiarity.

Nothing about the experience felt new.

The details were different. The location was different. The circumstances were different. Yet the emotional landscape felt remarkably similar to situations that had unfolded before. That realization proved difficult to ignore because it forced me to look beyond the events of a single day and examine a much larger story.

For years, I had viewed difficult moments as isolated incidents.

A disagreement would occur.

An apology would follow.

Time would pass.

Life would settle.

Then another situation would emerge, carrying different details but producing remarkably similar emotions.

Looking at each event individually made the larger pattern difficult to recognize. Every situation seemed capable of explanation. Every conflict appeared connected to a unique set of circumstances. Every difficult moment seemed separate from the ones that came before it.

The passage of time slowly challenged that belief.

Certain themes continued returning.

Fear often became frustration.

Frustration often became accusation.

Accusation often became anger.

Anger often gave way to apology.

The apology would create hope.

Hope would encourage patience.

Patience would carry the relationship forward until another cycle began.

Recognizing that pattern brought a different kind of pain than any individual incident ever could. Individual moments eventually pass. Patterns remain.

As those thoughts occupied my mind, other memories began surfacing.

I remembered nights when raised voices shattered the quiet that should have accompanied rest. I remembered moments when anger expressed itself through damaged property and broken objects. I remembered standing in situations that seemed completely disconnected from whatever event had originally caused the frustration. I remembered promises that things would improve and assurances that greater effort would be made moving forward.

Some of those promises were sincere.

I believe that.

Some apologies were sincere as well.

I believe that too.

The difficulty was never determining whether the words were sincere in the moment. The difficulty emerged when the same struggles continued returning despite the sincerity of those words.

That realization forced me to confront a question I had avoided for a long time.

How many times can a person explain away a pattern before honesty requires a different response?

The question unsettled me because I had spent years searching for explanations that would allow hope and reality to coexist without conflict. I wanted to believe the best. I wanted to extend grace. I wanted to support growth and healing wherever they appeared.

Those desires were genuine.

Yet another truth was becoming increasingly difficult to ignore.

Grace cannot erase reality.

Compassion cannot eliminate accountability.

Love cannot carry responsibilities that belong to someone else.

The longer I reflected upon those truths, the more I realized that my greatest struggle had never been understanding another person's behavior. My greatest struggle involved acknowledging the impact that behavior was having upon my own heart.

That recognition felt uncomfortable.

For years, my attention had remained focused outward. I spent far more time trying to understand another person's feelings than examining my own. Questions about their experience often received greater attention than questions about mine.

The events of that day changed something.

For the first time in a long time, I found myself asking a different question.

What has all of this been costing me?

The answer would take time to uncover.

The answer would require honesty.

The answer would require prayer.

Most of all, the answer would require me to recognize something Jesus had seen all along.

Love was carrying far more than I realized.
